# Additional Paper Notes (Skim)

## 1. Debate Only When Necessary: Adaptive Multiagent Collaboration for Efficient LLM Reasoning
**Eo et al. (2025), arXiv 2504.05047v2**

**Key contribution:** Proposes DOWN (Debate Only When Necessary), an adaptive multiagent debate framework that uses the confidence score of an agent's initial response to decide whether to activate debate. High-confidence responses skip debate entirely; low-confidence responses trigger a confidence-guided multiagent collaboration where agents refine answers by referencing peers' responses and their confidence scores. **Methodology:** A four-step pipeline: (1) initial response generation with confidence score extraction (via token-level softmax probabilities or verbalized confidence), (2) debate engagement check against a threshold, (3) confidence-guided multiagent collaboration (3 agents, 2 rounds), and (4) final answer via majority voting or judge agent. Threshold selection uses a Wilson-lower-bound scoring function balancing accuracy and efficiency. **Datasets:** MUSR (756 samples: murder mysteries, object placements, team allocation) and StrategyQA (229 samples). **Key results:** Achieves comparable or superior accuracy to full-debate baselines with up to 6x fewer agent calls (e.g., 1.4 average calls vs. 6.0 for Debate). Tested on Llama-3.1 8B, Ministral 8B, Qwen-2.5 72B, Llama-3.3 70B, and GPT-4o-mini. Importantly, DOWN mitigates error propagation from unnecessary debate -- the correct-to-incorrect flip rate is much lower than in MAD or standard Debate. **Relevance:** Directly relevant to Collaborative RLVR -- demonstrates that selective/adaptive collaboration is more effective than always-on debate, providing a principled mechanism (confidence thresholding) for deciding when agents should collaborate. This informs when and how to trigger collaborative reasoning during RLVR training.

## 2. Self-Improvement of Language Models by Post-Training on Multi-Agent Debate (MACA)
**Samanta et al. (2026), arXiv 2509.15172v3**

**Key contribution:** Introduces Multi-Agent Consensus Alignment (MACA), an RL framework that post-trains LMs using debate-derived consensus signals to improve self-consistency and collaborative reasoning. Shows that multi-agent debate provides richer training signals than single-round majority voting. **Methodology:** M clones of a base model engage in multi-round debate. After debate, responses are partitioned into majority (consensus-supporting) and minority (dissenting) trajectories. Four post-training objectives are compared: MV-SFT (imitate majority traces), MV-GRPO (online RL with consensus rewards), MV-DPO (preference learning on majority vs. minority pairs), and MV-KTO (unpaired preference learning). Preference learning (DPO/KTO) over full reasoning traces is most effective. No ground-truth labels needed. **Datasets:** MATH, GSM8K, MathQA (training); SVAMP, GPQA, CommonsenseQA (generalization). Models: Qwen-2B, Llama-3B, Phi-4B, Llama-8B. **Key results:** Multi-agent debate accuracy improves up to +26.87% (MATH) and +42.73% (MathQA, Llama-3B). Single-agent zero-shot accuracy improves up to +23.07% (MATH). Self-consistency improves up to +27.6% (GSM8K). Strong generalization to unseen benchmarks (+16.3% GPQA, +11.6% CommonsenseQA). MV-DPO/MV-KTO consistently outperform MV-GRPO and MV-SFT. **Relevance:** Highly relevant -- this is perhaps the closest paper to "Collaborative RLVR for Robust Reasoning." It demonstrates that multi-agent debate signals can be internalized via RL post-training, creating a self-improvement loop. The preference-learning approach (DPO/KTO on debate traces) and the consensus-as-reward paradigm are directly applicable design choices for collaborative RLVR.

## 3. SimpleRL-Zoo: Investigating and Taming Zero Reinforcement Learning for Open Base Models in the Wild
**Zeng, Huang, Liu et al. (2025), arXiv 2503.18892v3**

**Key contribution:** Provides a systematic investigation of zero RL training (RL directly from base models with rule-based rewards, no SFT) across 10 diverse open base models spanning different families (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek-Math, Qwen) and sizes (0.5B-32B). Identifies critical design factors and debunks the assumption that increased response length always corresponds to emergent reasoning behaviors. **Methodology:** Uses GRPO with rule-based correctness rewards (+1/0) on GSM8K and MATH training sets (~8K problems). Avoids format-based rewards. Monitors not just accuracy and response length but also reasoning behavior ratio (backtracking, verification, subgoal setting, enumeration via GPT-4o annotation), clip ratio, and average stopped length. Training data difficulty is matched to model capability (Easy/Medium/Hard splits). **Datasets:** Training: GSM8K + MATH. Evaluation: GSM8K, MATH500, Minerva Math, OlympiadBench, AIME24, AMC23, IFEVAL, MMLU, GPQA-Diamond. **Key results:** All 10 models show accuracy improvements. Response length increases for 9/10 models. Key findings: (1) increased response length does not always indicate genuine "aha moment" reasoning emergence -- Qwen2.5 models show length growth without increased verification behavior; (2) first observation of genuine cognitive behavior emergence (verification) in non-Qwen small models (Llama3-8B, DeepSeek-Math-7B); (3) rigid format rewards penalize exploration; (4) training data difficulty must match model capability; (5) zero RL training lifts pass@k by 10-30 points (not just reranking); (6) SFT cold-start limits post-RL exploration and performance. **Relevance:** Directly relevant to understanding how RLVR works across diverse base models. Key insight for collaborative RLVR: the quality of emergent reasoning varies dramatically across model families, and collaborative training may help weaker models benefit from stronger models' reasoning patterns. The finding that SFT limits RL exploration is important for pipeline design.

## 4. Continual Deep Reinforcement Learning with Task-Agnostic Policy Distillation (TAPD)
**Hafez & Erekmen (2024), arXiv 2411.16532v1**

*Note: This file was labeled as "DeepSeekMath / R1-Zero precursor" but actually contains a different paper on continual RL for Atari games.*

**Key contribution:** Introduces TAPD, a continual RL framework that adds a task-agnostic exploration phase (driven by intrinsic motivation/curiosity) to the existing Progress & Compress framework. The agent explores without task-specific rewards, distills gained knowledge, and later leverages it for faster downstream task learning. **Methodology:** Three-phase continual RL: (1) task-agnostic phase with curiosity-driven self-supervised exploration and periodic knowledge distillation, (2) progress phase for task-specific learning using lateral connections from a knowledge base, (3) compress phase for consolidating new knowledge into the knowledge base via EWC regularization. **Datasets:** Atari 2600 games from the Arcade Learning Environment (5 sequential tasks). **Key results:** Improved sample efficiency and forward transfer compared to baseline Progress & Compress approach. Addresses catastrophic forgetting without requiring task labels. **Relevance:** Limited direct relevance. The general principle of task-agnostic exploration distillation shares a conceptual link with how collaborative reasoning patterns might be learned in a task-general way before being applied to specific reasoning domains, but the domain (Atari RL) is quite different from LLM reasoning.

## 5. Do Language Models Know When They're Hallucinating References?
**Agrawal, Suzgun, Mackey, Kalai (2023), arXiv 2305.18248v2**

*Note: This file was labeled as "Measuring Faithfulness in Chain-of-Thought Reasoning" but actually contains a different paper on hallucinated references.*

**Key contribution:** Proposes hallucinated references as a tractable "drosophila" for studying LLM hallucination. Introduces indirect queries (asking for ancillary details like authors across independent sessions) as a complement to direct queries (asking if a reference is real) for detecting hallucinations using the same LM, without external resources. **Methodology:** Generate reference titles via LM prompts, label them as grounded/hallucinated using web search exact match. Compare three direct query templates (DQ1-DQ3: ask if reference exists) and indirect queries (IQ: ask for authors across independent sessions and measure consistency). All queries use the same LM in a black-box manner. **Datasets:** 1000 candidate paper titles per LM generated from 200 ACM CCS subconcepts (5 references each). Tested on GPT-3, ChatGPT, GPT-4. **Key results:** LMs often hallucinate different authors for fabricated references across sessions while consistently identifying authors of real references, suggesting hallucination is more a generation issue than a representation/training problem. Indirect queries provide useful discrimination between grounded and hallucinated references. **Relevance:** Modest relevance. The concept of using self-consistency signals (consistency across independent sessions) to detect unreliable outputs connects to how collaborative RLVR might use inter-agent agreement as a signal for reasoning quality. The generation-vs-representation framing of hallucination also informs whether collaborative debate can address reasoning failures.

## 6. Group-in-Group Policy Optimization for LLM Agent Training (GiGPO)
**Feng, Xue, Liu, An (2025), arXiv 2505.10978v1**

*Note: This file was labeled as "Chain-of-Thought Reasoning In The Wild Is Not Always Faithful" but actually contains a different paper on RL for LLM agents.*

**Key contribution:** Proposes GiGPO, a novel group-based RL algorithm that achieves fine-grained credit assignment for long-horizon LLM agent training while remaining critic-free, low-memory, and stable. Introduces a two-level (episode + step) advantage estimation structure. **Methodology:** Two-level advantage estimation: (1) Episode-level: groups of complete trajectories with macro relative advantages (like vanilla GRPO). (2) Step-level: an "anchor state grouping" mechanism identifies repeated environment states across trajectories and constructs step-level groups to compute micro relative advantages. Uses discounted returns for long-term impact. Final advantage is a weighted combination: A = A_E + omega * A_S. No extra rollouts needed -- uses only lightweight hashmap-based grouping. **Datasets:** ALFWorld (embodied household task planning) and WebShop (goal-driven web interactions), using Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. **Key results:** >12% improvement on ALFWorld and >9% on WebShop over GRPO baseline. Maintains same GPU memory overhead and identical LLM rollout cost (<0.002% additional time). Outperforms prompt-based agents, actor-critic baselines, and prior group-based RL methods. **Relevance:** Moderately relevant. The hierarchical credit assignment idea (episode-level + step-level advantages) could inform how to structure rewards in collaborative RLVR, especially for multi-step reasoning where different reasoning steps contribute unevenly to final correctness. The anchor state grouping concept has an analogue in collaborative RLVR: different agents encountering the same reasoning state could provide natural groups for credit assignment.
